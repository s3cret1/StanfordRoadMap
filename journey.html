How I Did It — My Journey (short & honest)

A real timeline and the decisions that mattered, translated into action steps you can follow.

Short version

I thought I had no shot: average extracurriculars, a so-so early SAT, and limited advising. I applied to CPS, secured a Matriculate advisor, kept improving my SAT, attended fly-ins, and rewrote essays until they reflected me. I matched to [Fill in the blank: Stanford].

Timeline & lessons (concrete)

Apply to CPS — Jan to Mar (Junior)

I almost didn’t submit; then I did. CPS connected me to peers, opportunities, and programs that mattered. Lesson: apply even if you feel unready.

Get a Matriculate advisor — March onward

My advisor enforced deadlines, reviewed my drafts, and offered admission-process guidance. Lesson: a trained second reader is high-impact.

Test & retest — Mar → Oct

My superscore grew with focused practice and timed exams. Lesson: tests are a lever—use official practice tools and schedule repeats.

Fly-ins — spring/fall

I applied to five—was rejected by some, accepted by others. Attending fly-ins led to interviews and improved fit. Lesson: apply widely; accept rejections as part of the process.

Write early & revise — summer → fall

I wrote in summer and revised relentlessly—honest reader feedback mattered most. Lesson: start early and iterate with tough readers.

Be kind to yourself — always

Impostor syndrome hits. Breaks helped me produce higher-quality work. Lesson: rest is strategic, not indulgent.

What I would do differently

  • Take one more year of science earlier to avoid senior-year anxiety about prerequisites.
  • Start SAT prep earlier to avoid last-minute pressure.
  • Apply to more affordable CPS-linked summer programs when possible.

Actionable checklist — first 30 days

  1. Finish CPS application (or check status). Deadline note: verify official dates.
  2. Take a diagnostic SAT/ACT and write a 4-week practice plan.
  3. Apply to Matriculate or a free advisor program.
  4. Draft a personal-statement outline and share with 1 honest reader.
